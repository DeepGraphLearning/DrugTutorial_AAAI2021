Artificial Intelligence for Drug Discovery, AAAI 2021

Keyworkds:
Artificial intelligence, machine learning, deep learning, drug discovery, graph representation learning, network medicine.

Jian Tang, Fei Wang, Feixiong Cheng

Abstract

Drug discovery is a long and costly process, taking on average 10 years and 2.5 billion dollars to develop a new drug. Artificial intelligence has the potential to significantly accelerate the process of drug discovery by analyzing a large amount of data generated in the biomedical domain such as bioassays, chemical experiments, and biomedical literature. Recently, there is a growing interesting in developing AI techniques for drug discovery in many different communities including machine learning, data mining, and biomedical community. In this tutorial, we will provide a detailed introduction to key problems in drug discovery such as molecular property prediction, de novo molecular design and molecular optimization, retrosynthesis reaction and prediction, and drug repurposing and combination, and also key technique advancements with artificial intelligence for these problems. This tutorial can be served as introduction materials for both computer scientist interested in drug discovery as well as drug discovery practitioners for learning the latest AI techniques along this direction.

Detailed description

Developing new drugs is a time-consuming and expensive process due to the low rate of success in clinical trials. As a large number of data are generated in the biomedical domain such as bioassays, chemical experiments, and biomedical scientific literature, recently there is a surge of interest in leveraging artificial intelligence for accelerating the process of drug discovery. A variety of studies have been conducted in different communities such as leveraging existing methods for new applications in biomedical community as well as developing new techniques for existing problems in machine learning and data mining community. However, a systematic introduction to the overall progress of AI for drug discovery including the key problems and the latest AI techniques in this domain is lacking, which could benefit researchers across different communities.

This tutorial aims to give a comprehensive introduction to the current progress of artificial intelligence for drug discovery. The main emphasis will be giving an introduction to the key problems in drug discovery as well as the latest machine learning techniques for these problems. The tutorial will be beneficial to both artificial intelligence researchers who are interested in drug discovery—who will be able to recognize important problems to work on through this tutorial—as well as drug discovery practitioners, who will learn the latest technical advancement.

The first part of the tutorial will be introducing some basics of drug discovery and artificial intelligence. We will begin the tutorial with an introduction to the overall process of drug discovery and then identify some fundamental machine learning problems such as molecule properties prediction (Gilmer et al., 2017), de novo molecule generation and optimization (Jin et al., 2018)(You et al., 2018), reaction prediction and retrosyntheis (Jin et al., 2017)(Shi et al., 2020), drug repurposing and combination (Zhou et al., 2020)(Cheng et al., 2018)(Cheng et al., 2019). Afterwards, we will give a brief introduction to some essential machine learning techniques for drug discovery, especially graph representation learning techniques (Sun et al., 2020).

The second part will give a detailed introduction to the latest AI techniques for each key problem introduced in the first part. For molecule properties prediction, we will introduce existing techniques based on graph neural networks or neural message passing framework (Gilmer et al., 2017), which are mainly trained in supervised fashion. Due to the limited amount of labeled data in drug discovery, there are recently increasing work on self-supervised (Hu et al., 2019)(Sun et al., 2020) or semi-supervised learning (Sun et al., 2020) techniques for molecule properties prediction. For molecular generation and optimization, different types of generative models for molecule generation will be introduced including the ones based on variational autoencoder (JTVAE (Jin et al., 2018)), autoregressive based methods (GCPN (You et al., 2018)), and the recent normalizing flow based methods (GraphAF (Shi et al., 2020), MoFlow (Zang & Wang, 2020)). As for retrosynthesis and reaction prediction, we will introduce both template-based (Jin et al., 2017)(Sacha et al., 2020) and template-independent approaches (Dai et al., 2019)(Schwaller et al., 2019)(Shi et al., 2020)(Sacha et al., 2020).

Besides approaches for developing new drugs, another important category of techniques for drug discovery is drug repurposing, which focuses on identifying existing approved drugs for treating new diseases. We will introduce traditional network-based approaches (Cheng et al., 2018) and recent approaches based on graph neural networks (Gysi et al., 2020). We will also introduce a case study on drug repurposing for COVID-19 (Zhou et al., 2020)(Gysi et al., 2020).

We will conclude the tutorial by introducing important data sources and data sets, discussing open challenges and pointing out future directions.

Tentative outline

The proposed tutorial would be 3.5 hours in length. An outline of the tutorial (including an approximate breakdown by presenter) is as follows:

  • Drug Discovery Overview [20 min, presented by Feixiong]
  • Deep Learning, Traditional Network-based Methods, Graph Representation Learning [15 min, presented by Fei]
    • Convolutional Neural Networks and Recurrent Neural Networks
    • Graph Convolutional Networks [GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), MPNN (Gilmer et al., 2017), GIN~(Xu et al., 2018)]
  • Molecule Properties Prediction [30 min, presented by Jian]
    • Variational autoencoder-based approach (Jin et al., 2018)
    • Autoregressive methods [GCPN (You et al., 2018)]
    • Normalizing Flow-based approaches [GraphAF (Shi et al., 2020), MoFlow (Zang & Wang, 2020)]
  • De novo Molecule Generation and Optimization. [40 min, presented by Jian and Fei]
    • Variational autoencoder-based approach [JTVAE (Jin et al., 2018)]
    • Autoregressive methods [GCPN (You et al., 2018)]
    • Normalizing Flow-based approaches [GraphAF (Shi et al., 2020), MoFlow (Zang & Wang, 2020)]
  • Reaction Prediction and Retrosynthesis. [30 min, presented by Jian]
    • Reaction prediction (Jin et al., 2017)(Schwaller et al., 2019)(Sacha et al., 2020)
      • Retrosynthesis (Dai et al., 2019)(Shi et al., 2020)(Sacha et al., 2020)
  • Multiomics and Clinical Data-based Drug Repurposing. [45 min, presented by Feixiong and Fei]
  • Other Topics. [15 min, presented by Fei]
  • Conclusion and Future Directions. [15 min, presented by Fei]

Goals and Intended Audience

Developing artificial intelligence techniques for drug discovery has dramatically increased in popularity over the past three years, with dozens of papers on the topic appearing at top venues—such as AAI, IJCAI, NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, and KDD—in the last year alone. These advancements are revolutionaizing a number of tasks in drug discovery, including molecule properties prediction, de novo molecular design and optimization, retrosynthesis prediction, to name a few. The popularity of AI for drug discovery across the machine learning, data mining, and biomedical communities has also led to an overwhelming diversity of closely related methods, and a tutorial synthesizing this diversity will be invaluable to AAAI researchers who are new to the area, while also being useful to experts who may not be familiar with connections between the various methodologies.

Prequisite knowledge The intended audience for the tutorial are participants that want to get up-to-speed with recent advancements in AI for drug discovery. The tutorial is targeted towards an entry-level audience, with knowledge of the fundamentals of machine learning and deep learning; e.g., empirical risk minimization and familiarity with LSTMS or CNNs. However, we expect that experts in the area will also benefit from the tutorial’s synthesis of disparate techniques and problems.

Learning outcomes The primary learning outcome will be to familiarize attendees with the key problems in drug discovery and imbue the audience with a high-level understanding of the conceptual relationships between these problems. A second key learning outcome will be learning the latest AI techniques for these problems and start to (re)implement standard algorithms in this space with the data sources and open source codes provided in this tutorial.
